Before your puppy comes home, get your supplies ready so you're not scrambling. The basics include a crate sized for your puppy's adult weight with a divider, food and water bowls, a high quality puppy food, a collar and leash, an ID tag, puppy safe chew toys, an enzyme based cleaner for accidents, and a comfortable bed. Having everything set up ahead of time makes the first day go much more smoothly.

Puppy proof your home before your puppy arrives, just like you'd baby proof for a toddler. Get down to puppy height and look for hazards: electrical cords, toxic plants, small objects they could swallow, cleaning chemicals, and anything chewable you care about. Block off rooms or areas you can't supervise. The less your puppy can get into trouble, the fewer bad habits you'll have to undo later.

Plan to bring your puppy home at a time when you can be around for the first several days. Most puppies are ready to leave their mother and littermates around 8 weeks old. The transition to a new home is a big deal for a young puppy, and having you present helps them settle and lets you start good routines right away. A long weekend or some time off is ideal.

Expect your puppy to be a little overwhelmed at first. Everything is new: the smells, the sounds, the people, and the absence of their mom and littermates. It's normal for a new puppy to seem scared, clingy, or unsettled for the first few weeks, and it can take a while for them to fully adjust. Keep things calm and low key at the start rather than overwhelming them with visitors and activity.

Establish routines from day one. Consistent times for meals, potty breaks, naps, play, and bedtime help your puppy feel secure and make house training and crate training go faster. Puppies thrive on predictability. Starting the schedule you actually want to keep, right from the first day, saves you from having to change habits later.

The first few nights are often the hardest, and some crying is normal as your puppy adjusts to sleeping alone for the first time. Placing the crate next to your bed for those first nights helps, since your puppy can see, hear, and smell you, and you'll notice when they need to go out overnight. Take them out for a potty break if they wake, but keep it calm and boring so they learn nighttime isn't playtime.

Schedule your puppy's first vet visit within the first few days to a week of bringing them home. The vet will do a full physical exam, check for parasites, start or continue the vaccination series, and set you up with parasite prevention and a deworming plan. This first visit is also your chance to ask all your new owner questions and get a health baseline for your puppy.

Be patient with yourself and your puppy. The first week is an adjustment for everyone, and it's normal to feel a little overwhelmed too. Lots of patience, consistency, and positive reinforcement during these early days lays the groundwork for a confident, well behaved adult dog. Things get easier as routines settle in and your puppy learns what to expect.
