Before your puppy comes home, get your supplies ready so you're not scrambling on day one. The basics include a crate sized for your puppy's adult weight with a divider, food and water bowls (stainless steel or ceramic are easiest to keep clean), a bag of the same puppy food the breeder or shelter has been feeding, a flat collar and a standard leash, an ID tag with your phone number, puppy-safe chew toys, an enzyme-based cleaner for accidents, grooming basics, and a comfortable bed. Having everything set up ahead of time makes the first day go much more smoothly.

Puppy-proof your home before your puppy arrives, just like you'd baby-proof for a toddler. Get down to puppy height and look for hazards: electrical cords, dangling blind cords, toxic houseplants, small objects they could swallow, medications, cleaning chemicals, and anything chewable you care about. Use baby gates to block off rooms you can't supervise, secure cabinets, and tuck cords out of reach. The less trouble your puppy can physically get into, the fewer bad habits and dangers you'll have to manage later.

Plan to bring your puppy home at a time when you can be around for the first several days. Most puppies are ready to leave their mother and littermates at around 8 weeks old, and leaving much earlier than that can cost them important early lessons in bite inhibition and dog social skills. The transition to a new home is a big deal for a young puppy, and having you present helps them settle, lets you start good routines immediately, and means you catch potty needs early. A long weekend or some time off to start is ideal, but avoid taking so much time that your absence later comes as a shock.

Ask the breeder, shelter, or foster for key information when you pick your puppy up. Find out exactly what food they've been eating and on what schedule, what vaccinations and dewormings have been done and when the next are due, whether they've been microchipped, and any health records. Bringing home a few days' supply of the current food lets you transition gradually to a new diet over a week or so instead of causing stomach upset with an abrupt switch.

Make the ride home calm and safe. Secure your puppy in a crate or a crash-tested harness rather than loose in the car or in someone's arms, bring towels in case of car sickness or accidents, and consider having a passenger sit nearby to reassure them. Some puppies get carsick or anxious on early rides, so keep the first trip short and low-key, and start building positive associations with the car right away through short, pleasant outings.

Expect your puppy to be a little overwhelmed at first. Everything is new: the smells, the sounds, the people, and the sudden absence of their mom and littermates. It's normal for a new puppy to seem scared, clingy, sleepy, or unsettled for the first few days to weeks, and some won't eat much the very first day. Keep things calm and low-key at the start rather than overwhelming them with a houseful of visitors and nonstop activity, and give them a quiet space to retreat to.

Introduce the home gradually rather than giving free run of the house. Start your puppy in one puppy-proofed room or a small gated area where you can supervise closely, with their crate, bed, water, and toys. A smaller world is easier for a young puppy to handle and makes house training far easier, since you can watch for potty signals. Expand their access room by room as they earn it by being reliable.

Establish routines from day one. Consistent times for meals, potty breaks, naps, play, and bedtime help your puppy feel secure and make house training and crate training go faster. Puppies thrive on predictability, and the routine you start on the first day is the one your puppy will come to expect, so begin the schedule you actually want to keep rather than one you'll have to change. Build in plenty of nap time, since young puppies need a great deal of sleep, often 18 to 20 hours a day, and an overtired puppy gets cranky and nippy.

The first few nights are often the hardest, and some crying is normal as your puppy adjusts to sleeping alone for the first time in their life. Placing the crate next to your bed for those first nights helps a great deal, since your puppy can see, hear, and smell you, settles faster, and you'll notice when they genuinely need to go out. Take them out for a potty break if they wake during the night, but keep it calm, quiet, and boring with no play, so they learn that nighttime is for sleeping.

Plan introductions to existing pets carefully and slowly. Introduce a resident dog on neutral ground like a walk rather than face-to-face in the home, keep early interactions short and supervised, and give your older pet plenty of breaks and their own space away from the puppy. Cats need their own escape routes and elevated spots, and should never be cornered by an excited puppy. Never leave a new puppy unsupervised with other pets until you're confident they all get along, and protect your older pets from being pestered.

Supervise interactions with children and teach gentle handling. Show kids how to pet calmly, not to chase, grab, hug tightly, or disturb the puppy while it's eating or sleeping, and teach them to give the puppy space. Young children and puppies should always be supervised together, both for the child's safety and so the puppy isn't frightened or hurt. Giving the puppy a child-free zone, like its crate or pen, helps everyone.

Schedule your puppy's first vet visit within the first few days to a week of bringing them home. The vet will do a full physical exam, check a stool sample for parasites, start or continue the vaccination and deworming series, discuss flea, tick, and heartworm prevention, confirm or place a microchip, and talk through spay or neuter timing. This first visit is also your chance to ask all your new-owner questions and establish a health baseline, and making it a pleasant, treat-filled trip helps your puppy feel good about the vet for years to come.

Be patient with yourself as well as your puppy. The first week is an adjustment for everyone, and it's completely normal to feel tired or even a little overwhelmed, sometimes called the "puppy blues." Lots of patience, consistency, and positive reinforcement during these early days lays the groundwork for a confident, well-behaved adult dog, and things get noticeably easier as routines settle in and your puppy learns what to expect.
