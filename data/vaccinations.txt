Puppies are born with immature immune systems and rely on antibodies they get from their mother's first milk, called colostrum. That early borrowed protection, known as maternal antibody, fades over the first few months, so puppies have to start building their own immunity through a series of vaccinations. This is why a single shot isn't enough. Maternal antibody can also block a vaccine from working if it's still present, and because it wears off at an unpredictable age in each puppy, the doses are repeated to make sure the puppy is covered as soon as their own immune system can respond.

Most puppies get their first round of core vaccines around 6 to 8 weeks of age. After that, boosters are given every two to four weeks until the puppy is at least 16 weeks old. A typical puppy gets three or four rounds total before the series is complete. Your veterinarian will set the exact timeline based on your puppy's age, health, and lifestyle, and the most important dose is the final one given at or after 16 weeks, because by then maternal antibody has almost always faded enough for the vaccine to take.

Core vaccines are the ones recommended for essentially every dog regardless of where they live or how they spend their time, because they protect against diseases that are widespread, severe, or dangerous to people. The main combination shot is usually labeled DHPP or DAPP, which covers distemper, hepatitis (caused by canine adenovirus), parainfluenza, and parvovirus. Rabies is also a core vaccine and is required by law across the United States.

Under the 2022 AAHA canine vaccination guidelines, leptospirosis is now also classified as a core vaccine recommended for all dogs, a change from older guidance that treated it as optional. Leptospirosis is a bacterial disease spread through the urine of wildlife and standing water, it can cause kidney and liver failure, and it can spread to humans, which is part of why recommendations shifted. Your vet will still tailor the final plan to your dog, but don't be surprised to see lepto included in the core series.

The rabies vaccine is usually given once the puppy is at least 12 to 16 weeks old, and all licensed products are labeled for puppies three months of age and older. State and local laws set the exact timing and the required interval, so ask your vet what your area requires. After the first rabies shot, a booster is due one year later regardless of whether the vaccine is a one-year or three-year formulation, and then it's repeated every one to three years depending on the product and local law.

Non-core vaccines are recommended for some dogs based on lifestyle, geography, and risk of exposure. These include Bordetella and canine parainfluenza (kennel cough), canine influenza, Lyme disease, and, in some regions, rattlesnake toxoid. If your puppy will be around a lot of other dogs at daycare, boarding, grooming, training classes, or dog parks, or if you live in an area with ticks or known disease outbreaks, your vet may recommend some of these. Many boarding and daycare facilities require the Bordetella vaccine.

Parvovirus deserves special attention because it is common, it spreads easily, and it is often fatal in unvaccinated puppies. The virus is extremely hardy and can survive in the environment for months, which is why young, partially vaccinated puppies are so vulnerable in public places. Until the series is complete, treat parvo as a real and present danger rather than a remote possibility, and be strict about where you let your puppy go and which dogs they meet.

Your puppy isn't considered fully protected until about a week or two after the final round of the core series, which usually lands somewhere around 16 to 18 weeks of age. Until then, it's safest to avoid high-risk public places like dog parks, pet stores, rest stops, and busy sidewalks where unvaccinated dogs may have been. This does not mean skipping socialization, which is also critical at this age; it means socializing carefully, in controlled settings and with healthy, vaccinated dogs you know.

Vaccine side effects are usually mild when they happen at all. You might notice some soreness or a small lump at the injection site, mild tiredness, reduced appetite, or a low-grade fever for a day or so. These typically resolve on their own within a day or two. Serious allergic reactions are rare but can include facial or muzzle swelling, hives, persistent vomiting or diarrhea, weakness, or trouble breathing, usually within minutes to hours of the shot. If you see any of those signs, contact your vet right away, as a severe reaction is an emergency.

If your puppy misses a scheduled vaccine, don't panic and don't assume you have to start the whole series over. Your vet will adjust the schedule to get your puppy back on track, though a long gap may mean an extra booster to be safe. The important thing is to reschedule as soon as you can, since gaps in the series leave your puppy vulnerable during a high-risk stage of life.

Keep a written or digital record of every vaccine, including the date and which product was given. You'll need proof of rabies vaccination for licensing, travel, boarding, daycare, and grooming, and a clear history helps any new vet pick up your puppy's care without guessing. Many clinics provide a vaccine card or an app, and it's worth keeping your own copy as a backup.

Once the puppy series is done, your dog moves to an adult vaccination schedule. The core combination shot and rabies are boosted about a year after the puppy series finishes, and then repeated every one to three years depending on the vaccine and local law. Non-core vaccines such as leptospirosis, Bordetella, Lyme, and canine influenza generally need annual boosters if your vet recommends them for your dog's lifestyle. Your vet may also discuss antibody titer testing in some situations as a way to check existing immunity, though titers do not replace legally required rabies vaccination.
