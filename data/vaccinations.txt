Puppies are born with immature immune systems and rely on antibodies they get from their mother's milk. That early protection fades over the first few months, so puppies have to start building their own immunity through a series of vaccinations. This is why a single shot isn't enough. The doses are spaced out so the puppy stays protected as the mother's antibodies wear off.

Most puppies get their first round of core vaccines around 6 to 8 weeks of age. After that, boosters are given every two to four weeks until the puppy is at least 16 weeks old. A typical puppy gets three or four rounds total before the series is complete. Your vet will set the exact timeline based on your puppy's age, health, and lifestyle.

The core vaccines are the ones recommended for every puppy regardless of where they live or how they spend their time. These protect against the most dangerous and widespread diseases. The main combination shot is usually called DHPP or DAPP, which covers distemper, hepatitis (adenovirus), parainfluenza, and parvovirus. Rabies is also a core vaccine and is required by law across the United States.

The rabies vaccine is usually given once the puppy is at least 12 to 16 weeks old. State laws vary on the exact timing, so ask your vet what your state requires. After the first rabies shot, the next one is due a year later, and then it's typically given every one to three years depending on local regulations.

Non core vaccines are optional and depend on your puppy's lifestyle and risk of exposure. These include Bordetella (kennel cough), Lyme disease, canine influenza, and leptospirosis. If your puppy is going to be around a lot of other dogs at daycare, boarding, or the groomer, or if you live in an area with ticks or known outbreaks, your vet may recommend some of these.

Your puppy isn't considered fully protected until about a week or two after the final round of shots, which usually lands somewhere around 16 to 18 weeks of age. Until then, it's safest to avoid high risk public places like dog parks, pet stores, and busy sidewalks where unvaccinated dogs may have been. Controlled playdates with healthy, vaccinated dogs you know are a safer way to socialize during this window.

Vaccine side effects are usually mild when they happen at all. You might notice some soreness at the injection site, mild tiredness, or a low grade fever for a day or so. Serious reactions are rare but can include facial swelling, vomiting, diarrhea, or trouble breathing. If you see anything that worries you, call your vet right away.

If your puppy misses a scheduled vaccine, don't panic and don't assume you have to start over. Your vet will adjust the schedule to get your puppy back on track. The important thing is to reschedule as soon as you can, since gaps in the series leave your puppy vulnerable during a high risk stage of life.

Once the puppy series is done, your dog moves to an adult vaccination schedule. The core combination shot and rabies are boosted about a year after the puppy series finishes, then repeated every one to three years. Non core vaccines like leptospirosis and Bordetella usually need annual boosters if your vet recommends them for your dog.
