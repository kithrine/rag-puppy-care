# Feeding Your Puppy

Puppies need food specifically formulated for growth, not adult dog food. Puppy food is more energy dense and has the right balance of protein, fat, calcium, and phosphorus to fuel rapid development and healthy bones. Feeding adult food to a puppy can lead to nutritional gaps during the most important growth period of their life. The single most useful thing to look for on the bag is the AAFCO nutritional adequacy statement, which should say the food is complete and balanced for "growth" or for "all life stages." That statement is your assurance that the food meets established minimums for a growing dog.

Read the ingredient label with a few simple priorities. A named animal protein, such as chicken, beef, or salmon, should be at or near the top of the list rather than a vague term like "meat by-products" or a pile of fillers. Beyond that, fancy marketing words like "premium," "holistic," or "human grade" are not regulated and don't guarantee quality. The AAFCO growth statement and a sensible ingredient list tell you far more than the front-of-bag claims.

How often you feed depends mostly on age. From weaning until about 12 weeks, puppies do best on four small meals a day because they have tiny stomachs and high energy needs. From around 3 to 6 months you can usually drop to three meals a day. By 6 months to a year most puppies transition to two meals a day, which is the schedule they'll keep as adults. Large and giant breeds sometimes shift on a slightly different timeline, so check with your vet.

Feed at the same times every day. A consistent feeding routine doesn't just regulate digestion, it also makes potty training much easier since puppies usually need to go 15 to 30 minutes after eating. A simple approach is to feed your puppy around the times you eat, at breakfast, lunch, and dinner. Feed the last meal early enough in the evening that your puppy has time to digest and empty out before bedtime, which helps prevent overnight accidents.

Measure every meal instead of free feeding. Free feeding, where you leave food out all day, makes it hard to control portions and can lead to obesity, which is the single most common preventable health problem in dogs. Measured meals also let you notice right away if your puppy isn't eating normally, which is often one of the earliest signs of illness. Use an actual measuring cup or a kitchen scale rather than eyeballing it, and start from the guidelines on the food bag.

Portion size depends on your puppy's current weight, expected adult weight, and the specific food, so the bag's chart is only a starting point. A Labrador will need more than a small terrier at the same age. Adjust based on body condition rather than just the chart. A puppy at a healthy weight should have a visible waist when viewed from above, a belly that tucks up when viewed from the side, and ribs you can feel under a thin layer of fat without pressing hard. If you can't feel the ribs at all, cut back a little; if they're sharply visible, feed a bit more. Your vet can help you dial in the right amount and assess body condition at checkups.

Large and giant breeds need special attention to growth rate. Growing too fast puts stress on developing joints and is linked to orthopedic problems later in life, so the goal is steady, controlled growth rather than getting big as quickly as possible. Choose a food formulated for large-breed puppies, which keeps calcium and overall calorie density in a controlled range, and avoid over-supplementing calcium. AAFCO sets a maximum calcium level for foods intended for puppies expected to reach 70 pounds or more as adults, precisely because excess calcium during growth can interfere with healthy skeletal development.

Small and toy breeds have the opposite concern. They're prone to hypoglycemia, or low blood sugar, because they have very little reserve. Going too long between meals can trigger it, so toy breeds often need more frequent feeding and sometimes a small snack between meals, especially in the first few months. Signs of hypoglycemia include weakness, wobbliness, glazed eyes, and in severe cases collapse or seizures, and it is an emergency that needs immediate sugar (such as rubbing corn syrup on the gums) and a vet.

Be cautious with grain-free and "boutique" diets. The FDA has been investigating a possible link between certain grain-free diets, particularly those high in peas, lentils, chickpeas, and potatoes, and a serious heart condition called dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM). The science is not fully settled, but many veterinary cardiologists and nutritionists now recommend grain-inclusive diets from established manufacturers unless your dog has a specific, vet-diagnosed need to avoid grain. True grain allergies in dogs are quite rare. When in doubt, ask your vet which foods they trust.

Be similarly cautious with raw and homemade diets. Raw diets carry a risk of bacterial contamination (such as Salmonella) for both the dog and the household, and homemade diets are very easy to get wrong nutritionally, especially the calcium-to-phosphorus balance that a growing puppy depends on. If you want to home-cook, work with a board-certified veterinary nutritionist to build a recipe that is actually complete and balanced rather than following a recipe off the internet.

When it's time to switch from puppy food to adult food, do it gradually. Transition over about 7 to 10 days by mixing in a little more of the new food and a little less of the old each day. A sudden change can upset your puppy's stomach and cause diarrhea. This same gradual approach applies any time you change foods, not just at the puppy-to-adult switch. The timing of that switch depends on breed size, since small breeds reach adult size around 10 to 12 months while large and giant breeds may stay on puppy food until 18 to 24 months.

Account for treats and don't let them crowd out balanced nutrition. A common guideline is that treats should make up no more than about 10 percent of your puppy's daily calories, with the rest coming from their complete and balanced food. If you train a lot, you can set aside part of your puppy's daily kibble to use as rewards so they aren't overfed. Keep training treats tiny; a puppy works just as happily for a pea-sized piece as a large one.

Avoid feeding table scraps and rich human food, which can upset your puppy's digestion, unbalance their diet, and encourage begging. Some common human foods are not just unhealthy but genuinely toxic to dogs, including chocolate, grapes and raisins, onions and garlic, and anything sweetened with xylitol. Keep a clear separation between people food and dog food, and make sure everyone in the household knows the rules.

Always keep fresh, clean water available and wash the bowls regularly. Bacteria and biofilm build up in food and water bowls even when they look clean, so wash them with soap and water daily or every couple of days. Stainless steel or ceramic bowls are easier to keep truly clean than plastic, which can scratch and harbor bacteria. Store dry food in a sealed container in a cool, dry place, and keep the original bag or its lot number in case of a recall.

Healthy puppies on a complete and balanced commercial diet generally do not need vitamin or mineral supplements, and adding them can do harm, especially calcium for large-breed puppies. If you think your puppy needs a supplement, ask your vet first. Watch for signs that a food isn't agreeing with your puppy, such as persistent loose stools, itchy skin, recurrent ear infections, or poor coat quality, and bring those up at your next visit so you can rule out a food sensitivity or an underlying problem.
