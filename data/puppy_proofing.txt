Puppy-proofing means going through your home and yard to remove or block off anything that could hurt your puppy or that your puppy could damage. Puppies explore the world with their mouths, have no sense of what's dangerous, and can move surprisingly fast, so the safest approach is to assume anything within reach will be chewed, swallowed, or knocked over. Doing this before your puppy arrives prevents accidents, protects your belongings, and reduces the chances of a frightening emergency.

Get down to your puppy's eye level, literally, to spot hazards you'd otherwise miss. Crawl through each room and look at what's reachable from the floor: dangling cords, low shelves, gaps behind furniture, and items left on the ground. What looks harmless from standing height can be an obvious temptation from a puppy's point of view. Tackle the rooms your puppy will actually use first, and use baby gates or pens to keep them out of areas you haven't secured.

Electrical and window cords are among the most serious hazards. A chewed electrical cord can cause burns or electrocution, so bundle cords, run them through cord protectors, unplug what you can, and route them out of reach. Cords from blinds and curtains pose a strangulation risk and should be tied up high. Phone chargers and earbuds left on low surfaces are easily grabbed and swallowed, so put them away.

Small objects are a choking and intestinal-blockage risk, and puppies will swallow astonishing things. Pick up coins, batteries, hair ties, rubber bands, jewelry, buttons, children's toys with small parts, sewing supplies, and anything else small enough to gulp. Batteries are especially dangerous because they can cause chemical burns if punctured. Keep floors clear, store small items in closed drawers or bins, and be especially vigilant in homes with young children whose toys end up everywhere.

Secure all chemicals, medications, and cleaning products. Human and pet medications, vitamins, cleaning supplies, laundry and dishwasher pods, antifreeze, pesticides, rodent and insect bait, and fertilizers can all be poisonous, and some are deadly in tiny amounts. Antifreeze is famously sweet-tasting and extremely toxic, and rodenticides are designed to be eaten, so store all of these in closed, elevated, or latched cabinets, and clean up any spills immediately. Keep purses and bags, which often hold gum, medications, or other hazards, up off the floor.

Know which plants are dangerous and move or remove them. A number of common houseplants and garden plants are toxic to dogs, including sago palm (extremely dangerous), lilies, oleander, azalea, dieffenbachia, pothos, and others, and bulbs and certain mushrooms in the yard can be harmful too. Either remove toxic plants or place them well out of reach, and supervise outdoor time so your puppy doesn't sample unknown plants or fungi. The ASPCA maintains a searchable list of toxic and non-toxic plants if you're unsure about a specific one.

Make the kitchen and bathroom safe, since both hold hidden dangers. In the kitchen, secure the trash behind a cabinet or use a latching lid (garbage holds toxic foods, bones, wrappers, and spoiled items), keep toxic foods like chocolate, grapes, onions, and anything with xylitol well out of reach, and watch the oven, stove, and dishwasher. In the bathroom, keep the toilet lid down, store medications and toiletries in closed cabinets, and put away soap, razors, and cotton swabs.

Protect your belongings and your puppy by managing temptations. Pick up shoes, socks, kids' toys, remotes, and anything chewable you care about, since these are both losses to you and choking or blockage risks to your puppy. Block access to furniture you don't want chewed and to rooms you can't supervise. Providing plenty of appropriate chew toys gives your puppy an acceptable outlet, which makes it far easier to keep them off the wrong things.

Don't forget the yard, garage, and outdoor spaces. Check fences for gaps, loose boards, and spots a small puppy could squeeze through or dig under, and make sure gates latch securely. Keep your puppy away from pools, ponds, and hot tubs unless supervised, and never assume a young puppy can swim. The garage often contains antifreeze, oil, paint, sharp tools, and pest poisons, so it should generally be off-limits, and check around and under cars before driving, since puppies may seek shade or warmth there.

Use management tools and supervision as your safety net, because no home is ever perfectly puppy-proof. A crate, an exercise pen, baby gates, and closed doors let you confine your puppy to safe, supervised spaces, especially when you can't watch them directly. Active supervision catches the things proofing misses, and confinement prevents trouble when supervision isn't possible. Finally, keep your veterinarian's number and a pet poison control hotline (such as the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center at 888-426-4435) handy, so that if your puppy does get into something, you can act quickly.
