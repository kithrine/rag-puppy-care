Dental care is one of the most overlooked parts of puppy care, yet it has a big impact on lifelong health. Dental disease is extremely common in dogs: by around three years of age, the majority of dogs already show some evidence of periodontal disease. Starting good dental habits in puppyhood, while the teeth are clean and the routine is novel, sets your dog up for healthier teeth and gums, fresher breath, and fewer painful and costly problems down the road.

The real enemy is plaque, not just visible tartar. Plaque is an invisible film of bacteria that constantly forms on the teeth above and below the gumline. If it isn't removed, it mineralizes into hard tartar and triggers inflammation of the gums (gingivitis), which can progress to deeper periodontal disease that destroys the tissue and bone anchoring the teeth, eventually causing pain, infection, and tooth loss. Because the worst damage happens below the gumline where you can't see it, prevention through regular cleaning matters more than how white the teeth look.

Brushing your dog's teeth is the single most effective thing you can do at home to control plaque between professional cleanings. Daily brushing is ideal, but brushing several times a week still helps meaningfully. Plaque begins to harden into tartar within a couple of days, so consistency is what counts. Use a soft dog toothbrush or a finger brush and toothpaste made specifically for dogs.

Never use human toothpaste on a dog. It often contains fluoride in amounts not meant to be swallowed and, importantly, may contain xylitol, a sweetener that is highly toxic to dogs. Dog toothpastes are made to be swallowed safely and come in flavors like poultry or beef that most dogs enjoy, which makes the whole process easier.

Introduce tooth brushing gradually so your puppy learns to accept and even enjoy it. Start by handling the muzzle and lifting the lips, rewarding calm acceptance, then let your puppy lick a little dog toothpaste off your finger so they like the taste. Next, rub a bit of paste along the gumline with your finger, then progress to a finger brush or soft toothbrush, building up over days to weeks. Keep sessions short and positive, focus on the outer surfaces of the teeth where plaque accumulates most, and aim the bristles at the gumline. Ending each session with praise or play keeps it something your puppy tolerates willingly.

Brushing is best started young and continued for life. While a puppy is teething and losing baby teeth (roughly 4 to 6 months), mouths can be tender, so be gentle and don't force it; the priority during teething is getting your puppy comfortable with mouth handling, with thorough brushing settling into routine once the adult teeth are in. The habit you build now is what makes lifelong dental care manageable.

Dental chews, special diets, water additives, and dental toys can support oral health, but choose products wisely. Look for the Veterinary Oral Health Council (VOHC) seal, which is given only to products shown by research to meaningfully reduce plaque or tartar. These aids supplement brushing rather than replace it. Be cautious with very hard chews such as antlers, hooves, bones, and hard nylon products, which are a leading cause of fractured teeth; a useful test is to avoid anything you couldn't dent with a fingernail or wouldn't want to be struck on the knee with.

Learn to recognize signs of dental trouble so you can act early. Warning signs include persistent bad breath, red, swollen, or bleeding gums, yellow-brown tartar buildup, drooling, pawing at the mouth, dropping food or chewing on one side, reluctance to eat hard food, and loose or discolored teeth. Dogs are good at hiding oral pain, so don't wait for your puppy to obviously struggle; mention any of these signs to your vet. During the puppy months, also watch for retained baby teeth that didn't fall out as adult teeth came in, which can crowd the mouth and trap bacteria.

Professional veterinary dental care complements your home routine. Your vet examines your dog's mouth at checkups and will recommend a professional cleaning when needed, which in dogs is done under anesthesia so the teeth can be cleaned below the gumline and X-rayed safely and thoroughly. Be wary of "anesthesia-free" dental cleanings, which mainly scrape visible surfaces and miss the disease below the gums where it matters. With consistent home brushing, smart use of VOHC-approved aids, and regular veterinary care, you can keep your dog's mouth healthy and comfortable for life.
