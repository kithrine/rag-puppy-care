Puppies are especially prone to parasites, both internal worms and external pests like fleas and ticks. Many puppies are born with or quickly pick up intestinal worms from their mother, and young immune systems are less able to keep parasites in check. A good parasite-prevention plan, set up with your veterinarian at the first visit, protects your puppy's health, prevents some parasites from spreading to people, and keeps your home from becoming infested. Year-round prevention is now the standard recommendation from veterinary parasitologists.

Intestinal worms are common in puppies, and the main ones are roundworms, hookworms, whipworms, and tapeworms. Roundworms and hookworms are so common that puppies are routinely dewormed on a schedule whether or not worms are seen. Hookworms can cause dangerous anemia in young puppies, and both roundworms and hookworms can infect humans, which is one reason prompt, repeated deworming matters. Tapeworms usually come from swallowing an infected flea or from hunting, and often show up as small rice-like segments around the tail or in the stool.

Follow a deworming schedule for young puppies. A common veterinary guideline is to deworm starting at about 2 weeks of age and repeat every 2 weeks through about 8 weeks (roughly at 2, 4, 6, and 8 weeks), then continue monthly until 6 months of age, typically through a monthly parasite-control product. Your vet sets the exact plan based on your puppy's history and a stool test. Because worms shed eggs intermittently, a single negative stool sample doesn't guarantee your puppy is worm-free, which is why routine deworming is used alongside testing.

Bring a fresh stool sample to checkups so your vet can screen for intestinal parasites. Veterinary guidelines suggest fecal examinations several times during the first year, since puppies are at higher risk and infections can be missed on any single test. If worms are found, your vet will prescribe the right dewormer for that specific parasite, since no single product treats them all. Always use veterinary-recommended dewormers rather than guessing with over-the-counter products, which may not cover the worm your puppy actually has.

Heartworm disease is serious, potentially fatal, and entirely preventable. It's spread by mosquito bites, the worms mature in the heart and lungs, and treatment in an infected dog is difficult, expensive, and hard on the dog, so prevention is far better than cure. Puppies should be started on a monthly heartworm preventive early (many products can begin by 6 to 8 weeks of age), and prevention is recommended year-round in most regions. Many monthly preventives conveniently also control intestinal worms like roundworms and hookworms.

Fleas are more than an itchy nuisance. They can cause allergic skin disease, transmit tapeworms, and in young or small puppies a heavy infestation can cause anemia from blood loss. Signs include scratching, biting at the skin, hair loss, and "flea dirt" (small black specks of digested blood) in the coat. Because most of a flea population lives in the environment rather than on the dog, controlling fleas usually means treating the puppy with a vet-approved product and cleaning the home (washing bedding, vacuuming thoroughly) at the same time.

Ticks transmit diseases such as Lyme disease, anaplasmosis, and ehrlichiosis, and they're a bigger risk in wooded, grassy, or brushy areas and in certain regions and seasons. After walks in tick country, run your hands through your puppy's coat and check common attachment spots like the head, ears, neck, armpits, between the toes, and around the tail. Remove any attached tick promptly with fine-tipped tweezers, grasping close to the skin and pulling straight out, then clean the area. A vet-recommended tick preventive plus routine checks is the best defense.

Use only species- and weight-appropriate, veterinary-recommended preventives, and never put a dog product on a cat or guess at dosing. Flea, tick, and heartworm prevention comes as monthly chewables, topical "spot-on" liquids, and longer-acting collars, and some combination products cover several parasites at once. Your vet will help you choose based on your puppy's age, weight, lifestyle, and local parasite risks, and will confirm the minimum age and weight for each product. Mark a monthly reminder so doses don't get skipped, since gaps reduce protection.

Some external parasites need specific attention. Mange is caused by mites: demodectic mange is common in puppies and often mild, while sarcoptic mange (scabies) is intensely itchy and contagious, including to people. Ear mites cause dark, crumbly ear debris and head shaking. Ringworm, despite the name, is a fungal skin infection, not a worm, and can spread to humans. Any persistent itching, hair loss, scaly skin, or ear trouble is worth a vet visit for an accurate diagnosis rather than home guesswork.

Good hygiene supports your prevention plan. Pick up feces promptly in your yard and on walks, since parasite eggs spread through stool and some can linger in soil. Wash your hands after handling stool, and teach children to wash up after playing with the puppy, because certain worms can infect humans. Keeping the yard clean, controlling fleas, and limiting your puppy's contact with unknown dogs' waste all reduce the parasite load your puppy is exposed to. With routine prevention, regular testing, and good hygiene, most parasite problems are easy to keep at bay.
