Start training your puppy as soon as you bring them home. Puppies can begin learning simple commands as early as 8 weeks old, and early training builds good habits before bad ones have a chance to form. Keep your expectations age appropriate, but don't wait. The first few months are a great time to lay a foundation.

The most effective and humane approach is positive reinforcement, which means rewarding the behaviors you want with treats, praise, or play. When your puppy does something right, mark it and reward it immediately so they connect the action with the good thing. Avoid punishment based methods, which tend to create fear and anxiety and can actually slow learning down. Dogs repeat what gets rewarded.

Keep training sessions short and frequent. A puppy's attention span is tiny, so five to ten minutes a few times a day works far better than one long session. End each session on a success while your puppy is still engaged and having fun. Short, upbeat sessions keep training something your puppy looks forward to.

Timing and consistency are everything. Reward the instant your puppy does the right thing, because even a few seconds' delay makes it harder for them to understand what they're being rewarded for. Use the same word and the same hand signal for each command every time, and make sure everyone in the household uses the same cues so your puppy isn't confused by mixed signals.

Sit is usually the easiest first command. Hold a treat just above your puppy's nose and slowly move it back over their head. As their nose follows the treat up, their bottom naturally lowers to the floor. The moment they sit, say "sit," reward, and praise. With a few repetitions your puppy starts to connect the word and the action.

Other foundational commands to work on early include come, down, stay, and leave it. Come is one of the most important for safety, so always make coming to you a positive, rewarding experience and never call your puppy to you for something they'll dislike. Leave it teaches impulse control and can keep your puppy away from dangerous things. Build these one at a time rather than all at once.

Use your puppy's meals and treats wisely. Training right before a meal, when your puppy is a little hungry and food motivated, makes them more eager to work. You can even use part of their daily kibble as training rewards so they aren't overfed. Mealtime itself is a good chance to practice sit and other behaviors.

Be patient and consistent, and remember that puppies don't learn everything overnight. There will be good days and frustrating days. Staying calm, upbeat, and consistent gets you there faster than getting frustrated. If you want extra help, a well run puppy class is a great way to get guidance and to work on commands around mild distractions.
