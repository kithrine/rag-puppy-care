Start training your puppy as soon as you bring them home. Puppies can begin learning simple commands as early as 8 weeks old, and early training builds good habits before bad ones have a chance to form. Keep your expectations age-appropriate, since a young puppy's focus and self-control are still developing, but don't wait to begin. The first few months are a great time to lay a foundation, and training is also a form of socialization and mental exercise that tires a puppy out and strengthens your bond.

The most effective and humane approach is positive reinforcement, which means rewarding the behaviors you want with treats, praise, or play, and simply preventing or ignoring the ones you don't. When your puppy does something right, mark it and reward it immediately so they connect the action with the good thing. Avoid punishment-based methods and aversive tools, which tend to create fear and anxiety, can damage your relationship, and often suppress warning signals rather than fixing the underlying behavior. Dogs, like all animals, repeat what gets rewarded.

Many trainers use a "marker" to make timing precise. A marker is a consistent sound, either a clicker or a short word like "yes," that you make at the exact instant your puppy does the right thing, followed quickly by a treat. The marker acts like a camera snapshot of the correct moment, bridging the tiny gap between the behavior and the reward so the puppy knows precisely what earned it. Charge up the marker first by clicking or saying "yes" and immediately treating a dozen times, until your puppy clearly connects the sound with food.

Keep training sessions short and frequent. A puppy's attention span is tiny, so five to ten minutes a few times a day works far better than one long session. End each session on a success while your puppy is still engaged and having fun, rather than drilling until they're bored or frustrated. Short, upbeat sessions sprinkled through the day keep training something your puppy looks forward to, and you can fold quick practice into everyday moments like before meals or before going outside.

Timing and consistency are everything. Reward within a second or two of the correct behavior, because even a few seconds' delay makes it harder for the puppy to understand what they're being rewarded for. Use the same word and the same hand signal for each command every time, say a cue only once rather than repeating it, and make sure everyone in the household uses identical cues and rules so your puppy isn't confused by mixed signals. Decide as a family, for example, whether "off" or "down" means get off the couch, and stick to it.

Sit is usually the easiest first command and a good place to learn the lure-and-reward method. Hold a treat just above your puppy's nose and slowly move it back over their head. As their nose follows the treat up and back, their bottom naturally lowers to the floor. The moment they sit, mark it, reward, and praise. After several repetitions, start saying "sit" right as they begin to sit, then gradually fade the food lure so the word and hand signal alone do the work.

Build a small set of foundational commands one at a time rather than all at once. Come, down, stay, and leave it are the most useful early on. Come (recall) is one of the most important for safety, so always make coming to you wildly positive and rewarding, practice it on a long line before trusting it off-leash, and never call your puppy to you for something they dislike like a bath or nail trim, or you'll teach them that coming when called is a bad bet. Leave it teaches impulse control and can keep your puppy away from dropped food, trash, or hazards.

Use your puppy's meals and treats strategically. Training right before a meal, when your puppy is a little hungry and food-motivated, makes them more eager to work, and you can set aside part of their daily kibble to use as rewards so they aren't overfed. Use higher-value treats, like small bits of chicken or cheese, for harder lessons or distracting environments, and save the everyday kibble for easy reps at home. Keep all training treats tiny so you can do many repetitions without filling your puppy up.

Generalize and proof behaviors gradually. A puppy who sits perfectly in your quiet kitchen often acts like they've never heard the word at the park, because dogs don't automatically apply what they learn in one place to every other place. Once a behavior is solid at home, practice it in slightly more distracting settings, then harder ones, raising the difficulty in small steps. Likewise, slowly stretch duration and distance for things like stay before expecting a long, far-away hold.

Prevent and redirect unwanted behaviors instead of just punishing them. For jumping up, turn away and ignore the puppy until four feet are on the floor, then reward calm greetings. For nipping, redirect to a chew toy and end play briefly if teeth meet skin. For counter-surfing or stealing items, manage the environment so the opportunity isn't there and trade for a treat rather than chasing. Teaching the puppy what to do instead, and rewarding it generously, works far better than scolding what you don't want.

Be patient and consistent, and remember that puppies don't learn in a straight line. There will be good days and frustrating days, and adolescence (often around 6 to 12 months) can bring a phase where a previously obedient puppy seems to forget everything; this is normal, and staying calm and consistent gets you through it. Getting frustrated or angry only makes your puppy anxious and slows learning. If you want extra guidance, a well-run puppy class that uses positive-reinforcement methods is a great way to practice around mild distractions and to catch problems early; for persistent issues, a certified trainer or a veterinary behaviorist can help.
