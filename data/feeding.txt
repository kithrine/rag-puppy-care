Puppies need food specifically formulated for growth, not adult dog food. Puppy food is more energy dense and has the right balance of protein, fat, calcium, and phosphorus to fuel rapid development and healthy bones. Feeding adult food to a puppy can lead to nutritional gaps. Look for a food with an AAFCO statement confirming it's complete and balanced for growth, with a real animal protein as the first ingredient.

How often you feed depends mostly on age. From weaning until about 12 weeks, puppies do best on four small meals a day because they have tiny stomachs and high energy needs. From around 3 to 6 months you can usually drop to three meals a day. By 6 months to a year most puppies transition to two meals a day, which is the schedule they'll keep as adults. Large and giant breeds sometimes shift on a slightly different timeline, so check with your vet.

Feed at the same times every day. A consistent feeding routine doesn't just regulate digestion, it also makes potty training much easier since puppies usually need to go 15 to 30 minutes after eating. A simple approach is to feed your puppy when you eat, at breakfast, lunch, and dinner. Feed the last meal early enough in the evening that your puppy has time to digest before bedtime, which helps prevent overnight accidents.

Measure every meal instead of free feeding. Free feeding, where you leave food out all day, makes it hard to control portions and can lead to obesity, which is the number one health problem for pets. Measured meals also let you notice right away if your puppy isn't eating normally, which can be an early sign of illness. Use an actual measuring cup rather than eyeballing it, and start from the guidelines on the food bag.

Portion size depends on your puppy's current weight, expected adult weight, and the specific food, so the bag's chart is only a starting point. A Labrador will need more than a small terrier at the same age. Adjust based on body condition rather than just the chart. A puppy at a healthy weight should have a visible waist tuck and ribs you can feel under a thin layer of fat. Your vet can help you dial in the right amount.

Small and toy breeds need extra attention because they're prone to hypoglycemia, or low blood sugar. Going too long between meals can cause it, so toy breeds often need more frequent feeding and sometimes a small snack between meals. Larger breeds have the opposite concern: growing too fast can stress developing joints, so controlled, measured portions matter even more for big dogs.

When it's time to switch from puppy food to adult food, do it gradually. Transition over about 7 to 10 days by mixing in a little more of the new food each day. A sudden change can upset your puppy's stomach. The timing of the switch depends on breed size, since small breeds reach adult size around 10 to 12 months while large and giant breeds may need puppy food until 18 to 24 months.

Always keep fresh water available and wash the bowls regularly. Bacteria builds up in food and water bowls even when they look clean, so wash them at least a couple times a week. Avoid feeding table scraps and human food, which can upset your puppy's digestion and encourage begging. If you use treats for training, keep them small and account for them so your puppy isn't overfed.
