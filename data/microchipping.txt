A microchip is a permanent form of identification for your puppy, about the size of a grain of rice, that is implanted just under the skin between the shoulder blades. Each chip carries a unique ID number that can be read by a scanner. Because collars can slip off and ID tags can break or become unreadable, a microchip serves as your puppy's reliable backup identification that stays with them for life. Large numbers of pets go missing each year, and a microchip dramatically improves the odds of a lost dog being reunited with its family.

Getting a puppy microchipped is quick and simple. A veterinarian inserts the chip with a needle, much like giving a vaccine, and most puppies barely react. It can be done during a routine visit and is commonly performed at the same time as spaying or neutering, while the puppy is already under anesthesia, though it does not require anesthesia on its own. Many breeders and shelters microchip puppies before they go to their new homes, so ask whether your puppy already has one.

It's important to understand what a microchip is and isn't. A microchip is not a GPS device and cannot track your dog's location in real time; it does nothing until a person scans it. What it provides is a permanent ID number that links to your contact information in a registry database. When a lost dog is brought to a veterinary clinic or animal shelter, staff scan it with a handheld reader, the chip's number appears, and they use that number to look up the registry and contact the owner.

Registration is the step that actually makes a microchip work, and it's the step people most often forget. A chip is useless if its number isn't linked to current contact information in a recovery database. After your puppy is chipped, make sure the chip is enrolled in a pet-recovery registry with your name, phone number, and address, and confirm who is listed as the owner (sometimes a breeder or shelter is initially listed and needs to be updated to you). Don't assume registration happened automatically; verify it.

Keep your registration information up to date, because an out-of-date record is one of the most common reasons microchips fail to reunite pets with owners. Whenever you move, change your phone number, or rehome a pet, update the registry promptly. It's also wise to list a second emergency contact in case you can't be reached. Make a note of your puppy's chip number and which registry it's in, and store it somewhere you can find it.

Have your puppy's chip checked periodically. Although it's rare, a chip can occasionally migrate slightly from its original spot or, very rarely, stop being detectable, so ask your vet to scan it during routine visits to confirm it's present and readable. This quick check costs nothing and gives you peace of mind that the chip will do its job if it's ever needed.

A microchip works best alongside, not instead of, a visible ID tag. A collar with a tag showing your phone number lets anyone who finds your dog contact you immediately, without needing a scanner, while the microchip provides permanent backup if the collar is lost. Using both gives your puppy the best chance of a fast reunion. Some areas also require dogs to be licensed, and a rabies tag on the collar provides additional identification.

Microchipping is increasingly expected and sometimes required. Many shelters and responsible breeders microchip as a matter of course, some jurisdictions require microchipping or licensing, and a permanent ID is needed for certain kinds of travel and for some registration and competition programs. Beyond any requirement, it's simply one of the most effective, low-cost things you can do to protect your puppy. A small, one-time step now means that if your dog ever slips out a door or gate, there's a permanent link leading them back home to you.
