Leash training teaches your puppy to walk calmly with you on a loose leash, which makes walks enjoyable and safe rather than a tug-of-war. Like other skills, it's easiest to build good habits early, before a small puppy grows into a strong dog that has practiced pulling for months. The aim isn't rigid heeling, but a relaxed walk where the leash stays slack and your puppy checks in with you and responds to direction.

Start with the right equipment. A flat collar or, better for many puppies, a well-fitted harness gives you a comfortable, safe connection; a front-clip harness in particular can reduce pulling without putting pressure on the throat. Use a standard fixed-length leash of about four to six feet for training. Avoid retractable leashes for teaching loose-leash walking, since they constantly reward pulling by giving more length, and avoid aversive tools like choke, prong, or shock collars, which can cause pain, fear, and injury, especially on a growing puppy. Make sure the collar or harness fits snugly enough that it can't slip off but isn't tight (the two-finger rule is a good check).

Let your puppy get comfortable wearing the gear before you go anywhere. Put the collar or harness on for short periods around the house paired with treats and play, so it predicts good things rather than feeling strange or scary. Then attach the leash and let your puppy drag it under supervision for a few minutes so the sensation of something trailing behind becomes normal. Some puppies freeze, bite the leash, or flop down at first; keep it light and rewarding and they'll move past it.

Begin training indoors or in a quiet, familiar space with no distractions. Reward your puppy generously for being near your side and for any slack in the leash. A simple method is to reward every time your puppy is at your leg, take a step, and reward again, so being beside you becomes the most rewarding place to be. Keep these first sessions very short and upbeat, ending while your puppy is still interested.

The core principle of loose-leash walking is that pulling never works. When the leash goes tight, stop walking and stand still like a tree, or calmly turn and go the other way, so your puppy learns that pulling makes forward progress stop and that a loose leash makes it continue. The instant the leash slackens or your puppy returns to your side, praise and reward and move on. This requires patience and consistency, because if pulling sometimes gets the puppy where they want to go, they'll keep trying it.

Use treats, your voice, and direction changes to keep your puppy engaged and oriented to you. Reward check-ins (when your puppy looks up at you), change direction frequently early on so your puppy learns to pay attention to where you're going, and keep a happy, encouraging tone. You can add a cue like "let's go" for starting to move and "this way" for turns. A puppy that finds you interesting and rewarding has little reason to drag ahead.

Build up to real-world distractions gradually. A puppy that walks beautifully in the living room will struggle at first in a busy park full of smells, dogs, and people, because those are powerful competing rewards. Add difficulty in small steps: from the house, to the yard, to a quiet street, to busier places, raising the challenge only as your puppy succeeds. Bring higher-value treats for harder environments, and don't expect the same focus outdoors that you get at home until you've practiced there.

Allow plenty of sniffing time, which is important for a dog's wellbeing and need not conflict with leash manners. Many trainers use sniffing as a reward, releasing the puppy with a cue like "go sniff" to investigate when the leash is loose. Distinguishing "working walk" time from "free sniff" time lets your puppy enjoy their nose while still learning not to pull. A walk that includes sniffing is more satisfying and tiring for your puppy than one focused only on covering distance.

Keep early walks appropriate for your puppy's age and vaccination status. Until the vaccine series is complete, do leash practice in safer areas rather than high-traffic dog spots, and remember that very young puppies tire quickly and shouldn't be marched long distances on hard surfaces while their joints are developing. Short, frequent, positive outings beat long, exhausting ones. Always carry waste bags and clean up after your puppy.

Be patient and consistent, and keep the whole experience positive. Leash skills take time and lots of repetition, and adolescence can bring a phase where a previously polite walker tests the limits again. Avoid yanking, dragging, or scolding, which make walks stressful and can teach your puppy to dread the leash; instead, reward what you want and manage the rest. If pulling or reactivity on leash becomes a real problem, a positive-reinforcement trainer can help you turn it around before it becomes an entrenched habit.
