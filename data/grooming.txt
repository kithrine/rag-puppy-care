Start grooming your puppy early, even before they really need it, so they get comfortable being handled. Regularly touching their paws, ears, mouth, and tail, and pairing it with treats and praise, makes future grooming and vet visits far less stressful. The goal at this age is less about the actual grooming and more about teaching your puppy that being handled is normal and pleasant.

How often you brush depends on your puppy's coat type. Short coated breeds may only need a quick brush once a week, while long or double coated breeds need brushing several times a week or even daily to prevent mats and tangles. Brushing also spreads natural oils through the coat, removes loose hair, and gives you a regular chance to check for fleas, ticks, lumps, or skin issues.

Puppies don't need frequent baths, and over bathing can dry out their skin. For most puppies, a bath every few weeks to a couple months is plenty, or whenever they get genuinely dirty. Use a shampoo made specifically for dogs, since human shampoo has the wrong pH for their skin. Keep the water lukewarm, avoid getting water in their ears, and make it a calm, positive experience.

Nail trimming is a part of grooming a lot of owners find intimidating, but it's important. Overgrown nails can be painful and affect how your dog walks. Trim a little at a time and avoid the quick, which is the pink area inside the nail that contains blood vessels and nerves. If your puppy has dark nails where you can't see the quick, trim just tiny amounts at a time. Getting your puppy used to having their paws handled early makes this much easier.

Dental care matters more than many new owners realize. Start getting your puppy used to having their teeth brushed with a dog specific toothpaste, never human toothpaste, which can be toxic to dogs. Daily or at least several times a week is ideal. Building this habit while they're young sets them up for better dental health and fewer problems as an adult.

Check and clean your puppy's ears regularly, especially for breeds with floppy ears that trap moisture. Use a vet recommended ear cleaner and a cotton ball, and never push anything deep into the ear canal. Signs of an ear problem include redness, a bad smell, dark discharge, or your puppy frequently scratching or shaking their head. If you see those, check with your vet.

Don't forget parasite prevention as part of routine care. Your vet will set you up with flea, tick, and heartworm prevention, and a deworming schedule to clear out intestinal parasites like roundworms and hookworms that are common in puppies. Staying on top of parasite prevention protects your puppy's health and keeps your home from getting infested.

Keep early grooming sessions short, gentle, and rewarding. If your puppy gets squirmy or stressed, stop while it's still a positive experience and try again later rather than forcing it. Lots of treats and praise during handling teaches your puppy that grooming is nothing to fear, which pays off for their whole life.
