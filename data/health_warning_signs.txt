Knowing what's normal for your puppy makes it much easier to spot when something is wrong. A healthy puppy is generally bright, alert, and playful when awake, eats and drinks with enthusiasm, has bright eyes and a clean nose, breathes quietly and easily, and has formed stools and normal urination. A normal body temperature for a dog is roughly 100.5 to 102.5 degrees Fahrenheit (about 38 to 39 degrees Celsius), taken rectally; anything above about 103 or below about 99 is a reason to call your vet. Because puppies have little reserve and can go downhill fast, it's better to call sooner rather than wait and see.

Some symptoms warrant a same-day or emergency call rather than monitoring at home. These include repeated vomiting or diarrhea (especially with blood), refusing food or water for more than a day, extreme lethargy or weakness, a distended or painful belly, difficulty breathing, pale or bluish gums, collapse, seizures, suspected poisoning, trouble urinating or defecating, a fever, or signs of significant pain. In a young puppy, the combination of not eating, vomiting, and diarrhea is a red flag that needs prompt veterinary attention.

Parvovirus is the illness new puppy owners most need to recognize, because it is common, spreads easily, and can kill quickly. Early signs are often subtle: a usually bouncy puppy becomes quiet, sleeps more, and loses interest in food, since the body is diverting energy to fight the virus. This progresses to repeated vomiting and profuse, often bloody diarrhea with a distinctive foul odor, along with rapid dehydration. In puppies, the slide from mild lethargy to severe illness can happen within 24 to 48 hours, so parvo is never a wait-and-see situation. With prompt, intensive veterinary treatment most puppies survive, but the key word is prompt.

Dehydration is dangerous in small puppies and can develop fast with vomiting or diarrhea. Signs include tacky or dry gums, sunken-looking eyes, lethargy, and loss of skin elasticity, which you can roughly check by gently lifting the skin over the shoulders and seeing if it springs back quickly (slow return suggests dehydration). Because puppies dehydrate quickly, ongoing vomiting or diarrhea, or refusing to drink, should prompt a vet call rather than home rehydration attempts alone.

Watch the gums as a quick health gauge. Healthy gums are usually pink and moist, and when you press a fingertip on them the color should return within about two seconds. Pale or white gums can signal blood loss, shock, or anemia; blue or gray gums signal a lack of oxygen; bright red gums can indicate overheating or illness; and yellow gums can suggest a liver problem. Any of these color changes is a reason to seek care promptly. Note that some breeds normally have pigmented (dark) gums, so learn what's normal for your puppy.

Toy and small-breed puppies are prone to hypoglycemia, or dangerously low blood sugar, especially when very young, stressed, or if they miss meals. Signs include weakness, wobbliness, glassy or unfocused eyes, trembling, and in severe cases collapse or seizures. If you see early signs, rub a little corn syrup or honey on the gums and get to a vet, and prevent it by feeding small, frequent meals. This is one reason regular feeding times matter so much for tiny puppies.

Heatstroke is a true emergency, and puppies, flat-faced (brachycephalic) breeds, and dogs left in warm cars or exercised in heat are at particular risk. Warning signs include heavy frantic panting, drooling, bright red gums, weakness, vomiting, collapse, and a body temperature that climbs dangerously high. Move the dog to shade or air conditioning, offer cool (not ice-cold) water, wet them with cool water, and head to a vet immediately. Never leave a puppy in a parked car, even briefly, and avoid exertion during the hottest parts of the day.

Keep an eye on breathing and coughing. Rapid, labored, or noisy breathing at rest, persistent coughing, or gagging can signal respiratory infection (such as kennel cough), pneumonia, or other problems, and breathing difficulty is always urgent. A normal resting puppy breathes quietly; counting resting breaths when your puppy is calm or asleep gives you a baseline (most dogs are well under about 30 breaths per minute at rest). Sudden increases in resting breathing rate or effort deserve a call.

Monitor stool and appetite as everyday health indicators. Occasional soft stool can come from a diet change or stress, but persistent diarrhea, worms or rice-like segments in the stool, straining, or blood are reasons to collect a sample and call your vet. A sudden, lasting drop in appetite is one of the most reliable early signs that a puppy isn't feeling well, which is another reason measured meals (where you notice exactly how much they eat) beat free feeding.

Know your emergency resources before you need them. Keep your regular vet's number and the address and number of the nearest 24-hour emergency animal hospital somewhere easy to find. For suspected poisoning, the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center (888-426-4435) and the Pet Poison Helpline (855-764-7661) are available around the clock, though a consultation fee may apply. When you call any of these, try to have your puppy's approximate weight, age, and details of what happened ready, and don't induce vomiting or give any home remedy unless a professional tells you to.

When in doubt, call. Puppies are small, change quickly, and can't tell you what's wrong, so it's far better to make a phone call that turns out to be nothing than to delay on something serious. Veterinary staff would much rather answer a question early than treat a crisis that could have been caught sooner. Trust your instincts: if your puppy seems "off" in a way that worries you, a call to your vet is always reasonable.
