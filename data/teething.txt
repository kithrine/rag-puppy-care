Puppies go through teething much like human babies do. They're born without teeth, get their baby teeth (also called milk or deciduous teeth) starting around 3 weeks of age, and then those fall out as the adult teeth come in. Most puppies have all 28 baby teeth by around 6 to 8 weeks, begin losing them around 4 months, and finish getting their 42 permanent adult teeth somewhere around 6 months of age. The heaviest teething and chewing usually happens between about 3 and 6 months, when baby teeth are loosening and adult teeth are pushing through.

Teething is uncomfortable, and chewing helps relieve the soreness in your puppy's gums. On top of that, puppies explore the world with their mouths the same way human infants use their hands, so mouthing and chewing is completely normal puppy behavior, not bad behavior or spite. Understanding that it's a developmental need rather than misbehavior changes how you respond: the goal isn't to stop the chewing, it's to redirect it onto appropriate things and to manage the environment so the wrong things aren't available.

Give your puppy plenty of safe, appropriate chew toys to satisfy the urge. Look for toys made specifically for puppies that are durable but have some give, since a puppy's teeth and developing jaws can be damaged by anything too hard. A good rule of thumb is to avoid chews so hard you couldn't dent them with a fingernail or wouldn't want to be hit on the knee with them, such as antlers, hooves, and hard nylon bones, because they can fracture teeth. Rotating a few different textures and shapes keeps things interesting and helps you learn what your puppy likes.

Cold can soothe sore gums. Many owners freeze a clean, wet washcloth (twisted and frozen so the puppy can gnaw it), a puppy-safe rubber toy, or a rubber toy stuffed with a little wet food and frozen, and the cold helps numb the discomfort. Supervise any frozen washcloth so your puppy chews rather than shreds and swallows it. Frozen or chilled chews can be especially helpful during the most intense weeks of teething.

When your puppy chews something they shouldn't, calmly redirect them to an appropriate toy rather than punishing them. The pattern is simple and consistent: gently interrupt, offer a chew toy, then praise and maybe reward them the moment they take it. Over time this teaches them what is okay to chew. Punishment after the fact doesn't teach the puppy what to do instead, can make them anxious, and often just teaches them to chew the forbidden item when you're not watching.

Manage the environment so your puppy simply can't reach the wrong things, the same way you'd baby-proof for a toddler. Keep shoes, socks, cords, remotes, kids' toys, and anything else tempting or dangerous out of reach. Block off areas you can't supervise, and use a crate or playpen with appropriate chews when you can't watch closely. Preventing access to forbidden items is far easier and more reliable than correcting after the fact, and it protects your puppy from swallowing something hazardous.

Play biting, where your puppy nips at your hands and clothing during play, is also part of this stage and is normal. Puppies learn bite inhibition, the ability to control the force of their mouth, from their mother and littermates, and you continue that lesson. When your puppy bites too hard, you can let out a brief, high-pitched "ouch" and stop play or step away for a moment, which teaches them that hard biting ends the fun. Then redirect to a toy so they learn that hands are not for chewing. Never encourage rough hand-wrestling games that teach the opposite lesson, and remember that an overtired puppy nips more, so a nap often helps.

Some chewing items are best avoided for safety. Cooked bones can splinter and cause choking or internal injury, and even some marketed chews like rawhide can pose choking or blockage risks, so supervise any chew and take away pieces small enough to swallow. Choose appropriately sized items so they can't be gulped, and check toys regularly for damage, discarding anything that's coming apart.

You'll often find tiny baby teeth around the house during teething, or you may never find them, since puppies frequently swallow them harmlessly while eating or chewing. A little blood on a chew toy, or faint pink saliva, is usually normal as teeth come loose during this stage. Bad breath during teething can occur as gums are irritated, but persistent foul breath, swollen or bleeding gums beyond the normal teething period, or signs of real pain are worth a vet visit.

Watch for retained baby teeth as the adult teeth come in. Sometimes an adult tooth erupts while the baby tooth it should replace hasn't fallen out, leaving two teeth in one spot, most often the upper canine ("fang") teeth. Retained baby teeth can crowd the adult teeth, trap food and bacteria, and cause bite or alignment problems, so if you notice a doubled-up tooth, or your puppy still has baby teeth by around 6 to 7 months, have your vet take a look. They can be removed, often at the same time as spay or neuter surgery, to prevent lasting dental problems.

Use the teething months to build lifelong dental habits. While you're already handling your puppy's mouth, start getting them used to having their teeth and gums touched and gently brushed with dog-specific toothpaste, so that good dental care feels routine by the time the adult teeth are fully in. Establishing the habit now, when you're paying attention to their mouth anyway, makes ongoing dental care much easier for the rest of their life.
