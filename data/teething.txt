Puppies go through teething just like human babies do. They're born without teeth, get their baby teeth (also called milk teeth) starting around 3 weeks, and then those fall out as the adult teeth come in. Most puppies have all 28 baby teeth by around 6 weeks and finish getting their 42 adult teeth somewhere around 6 months of age. The heaviest teething and chewing usually happens between about 3 and 6 months.

Teething is uncomfortable, and chewing helps relieve the soreness in your puppy's gums. On top of that, puppies explore the world with their mouths the same way babies do, so mouthing and chewing is completely normal puppy behavior, not bad behavior. Understanding that it's a need rather than misbehavior changes how you respond to it. The goal isn't to stop chewing, it's to redirect it onto appropriate things.

Give your puppy plenty of safe chew toys to satisfy the urge. Look for toys made specifically for puppies that are durable but have some give, since a puppy's teeth and jaws are still developing. Rotating a few different textures keeps things interesting. Some owners freeze a wet washcloth or a puppy safe rubber toy, and the cold helps numb sore gums and soothe the discomfort.

When your puppy chews something they shouldn't, calmly redirect them to an appropriate toy instead of punishing them. The pattern is simple: interrupt gently, then offer a chew toy, then praise them when they take it. Over time this teaches them what is okay to chew. Punishment doesn't teach the puppy what to do instead and can make them anxious.

Puppy proof your home the same way you'd baby proof for a toddler. Keep shoes, cords, remotes, and anything else tempting out of reach. Block off areas you can't supervise, and use a crate or playpen when you can't watch your puppy closely. Managing the environment so your puppy simply can't get to the wrong things is far easier than correcting after the fact.

Play biting, where your puppy nips at your hands during play, is also part of this stage. Puppies learn bite inhibition, which is how to control the force of their mouth, from their littermates and mother, and you continue that lesson. When your puppy bites too hard, you can let out a small yelp and stop play for a moment, which teaches them that hard biting ends the fun. Redirect to a toy so they learn hands aren't for chewing.

You'll often find tiny baby teeth around the house during teething, or you may never find them since puppies frequently swallow them harmlessly. A little blood on a chew toy is usually normal during this stage. That said, if you notice an adult tooth coming in while the baby tooth hasn't fallen out (called a retained tooth), or if your puppy seems to be in significant pain or has very bad breath, it's worth checking with your vet.
