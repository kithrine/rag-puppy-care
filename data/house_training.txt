House training works best when you put your puppy on a consistent schedule and stick to it. Puppies thrive on routine, and predictable meals, potty breaks, naps, and playtime all make it easier for them to learn where and when to go. It won't happen overnight, so patience and consistency matter more than anything else. The two pillars are supervision and routine: if you control when food goes in and watch closely for when it needs to come out, accidents become rare.

A young puppy can only hold its bladder for so long. A useful rule of thumb is the "month plus one" rule: take your puppy's age in months and add one, and that's roughly the maximum number of hours they can comfortably hold it. So an 8-week-old (2-month) puppy maxes out around three hours, a 12-week-old around four hours, and so on. Cap it around six to eight hours once they're an adult. During active daytime hours, young puppies need to go out far more often than this maximum, often every one to two hours.

There are predictable times your puppy will almost always need to potty. Take them out first thing in the morning, after every meal, after every nap, after play sessions, after drinking a lot of water, before and after crate time, and last thing before bed. After a meal the window is short, usually between 5 and 30 minutes, and the younger the puppy the sooner they'll need to go. Building the habit of "out the door at every transition" catches the vast majority of accidents before they happen.

Start every day the same way. When you wake up, get your puppy out of the crate and straight outside before you do anything else. Don't stop for coffee or to check your phone. With a small puppy you can carry them out so they don't have an accident on the way to the door. Always head out the same door to the same spot so the location becomes part of the routine, and so the lingering scent there reminds them what to do.

Go outside with your puppy rather than just letting them out, and keep them on a leash during training even in a fenced yard. This keeps them focused on the task instead of wandering off to play, and it lets you see exactly what happened so you can reward at the right moment. Wait quietly for them to go rather than turning it into a walk right away. You can add a gentle cue word like "go potty" as they start, so that eventually the phrase prompts them to go on command, which is invaluable in bad weather or on trips.

Reward immediately, outside, the instant your puppy finishes. Praise calmly and give a small treat right there, within a second or two, while they're still at the spot. If you wait until you're back inside to reward, the puppy connects the treat with coming indoors rather than with going potty outside. Make going in the right place clearly the most rewarding option, and they'll start to seek it out.

Learn to read your puppy's signals. Sudden quiet, circling, sniffing the floor intently, whining, restlessness, or heading toward a door are all signs they need to go. Most puppies start showing these pre-potty signals within a couple of weeks of consistent training. If you catch the signal and get them outside in time, every success builds the habit faster. Some owners teach a deliberate signal, like hanging bells on the door for the puppy to nudge, though it's optional.

Manage the environment so accidents are hard to have in the first place. When you can't actively watch your puppy, they should be in a crate, a playpen, or tethered near you rather than roaming the house unsupervised. Use baby gates to keep them in the room you're in. As your puppy proves reliable in one area, gradually expand their freedom room by room, rather than giving the whole house at once and hoping for the best.

Accidents are going to happen, and they're almost always a missed schedule or a lapse in supervision rather than a training failure or spite. Never punish your puppy for an accident and never rub their nose in it. Punishment-based methods actually slow house training down and can teach the puppy to hide when they go, or to be afraid to potty in front of you even outside. If you catch them mid-accident, calmly interrupt with a clap or an "oops," scoop them up, and get them outside to finish, then reward them there. If you find it after the fact, just clean it up and resolve to supervise more closely.

Clean accidents with an enzyme-based cleaner, not a regular household cleaner. Standard cleaners don't fully break down the proteins in urine, so even when the spot looks and smells clean to you, the puppy can still smell the old marker and will be drawn back to go there again. Enzyme cleaners specifically break those compounds down so the spot doesn't keep pulling them back. Avoid ammonia-based products in particular, since urine contains ammonia and the smell can actually attract repeat accidents.

Decide early whether you'll use potty pads, and understand the tradeoff. Pads can be useful for very young puppies, apartment living, or long workdays, but they also teach the puppy that going indoors is acceptable, which can slow down or confuse outdoor training. If you do use them, place them in a consistent spot and plan to phase them out by gradually moving them toward and then out the door. For most owners who want a reliably outdoor-trained dog, minimizing pad use leads to faster, cleaner results.

Most puppies are reliably house trained within a few weeks to a few months of consistent work, though it varies by breed, by size, and by how steady you are with the schedule. Small breeds often take longer because they have smaller bladders. A 6-month-old puppy is usually dependable most of the time, but expect occasional accidents until close to a year. If your puppy was doing well and suddenly regresses, or is having far more accidents than expected, drinking or urinating excessively, or straining, see your vet to rule out a urinary tract infection or other medical issue before assuming it's a training problem.
