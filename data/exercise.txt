Puppies need exercise to burn energy, build coordination, and stay healthy, but they need the right kind and amount for their stage of development. A growing puppy's body, especially the joints, is more vulnerable than an adult dog's, so the goal is regular, moderate, low-impact activity rather than long or intense workouts. Done well, exercise also doubles as training and socialization and helps prevent the boredom-driven chewing and restlessness that get many puppies into trouble.

A puppy's bones are still growing at soft areas near the ends called growth plates, which haven't yet hardened into solid bone. Until those plates close, the bones are more susceptible to injury from repetitive pounding, hard twisting, and high-impact landings. Growth plates close at different ages depending on size: roughly 6 to 12 months in small breeds and often 12 to 18 months or more in large and giant breeds. This is why big-breed puppies in particular need a careful, conservative approach to exercise for longer than you might expect.

A widely shared guideline is the "five-minute rule," which suggests about five minutes of structured exercise per month of age, once or twice a day, until a puppy is fully grown. By this rule a 3-month-old would get roughly 15 minutes and a 5-month-old about 25 minutes of formal walking per session. It's worth knowing that this rule is a rule of thumb rather than a scientifically proven formula, and it shouldn't be treated as a hard limit for every puppy. Use it as a sensible starting point, then watch your individual puppy and adjust.

The distinction the five-minute rule is really pointing at is between forced, repetitive, human-directed exercise and free play. Free play, where a puppy runs, stops, sniffs, and rests at its own pace in a safe area, is self-regulating: a puppy will usually slow down or flop over when tired. Forced exercise, like long leashed walks, jogging beside a person or bike, or endless fetch, doesn't let the puppy stop when their body needs a break, and that's the kind worth keeping moderate. Prioritize off-leash, self-paced play and short, easy walks over long-distance or high-repetition activity.

Protect growing joints by avoiding high-impact and repetitive stress while your puppy is still developing. Limit jumping from heights (off furniture, out of cars, over agility-style jumps), hard or twisting stops in fetch, jogging or running long distances on hard pavement, and forced stair climbing. You don't have to wrap your puppy in cotton wool, but routine pounding and big jumps before the growth plates close are linked to orthopedic problems later, so save high-impact sports until your vet confirms your dog is physically mature.

Mental exercise tires a puppy out as effectively as physical exercise, and it doesn't stress the joints. Food puzzles, snuffle mats, scatter-feeding in the grass, short training sessions, learning new tricks, and "find it" nose games all give a puppy a satisfying workout for the brain. A puppy that's mentally engaged is calmer and easier to live with, and on days when physical activity must be limited (bad weather, recovery, or just a young puppy) mental games pick up the slack.

Sniffing is a legitimate and valuable form of exercise. Letting your puppy stop and investigate smells on a "sniffari" walk, where the pace is set by the nose rather than by distance covered, is enriching and tiring in a good way. A slow, sniffy ten-minute walk can satisfy a puppy more than a brisk march of the same length. Build in plenty of sniffing time and resist the urge to make every walk about covering ground.

Read your puppy and don't overdo it. Signs a puppy has had enough include lagging behind, lying down and refusing to continue, excessive panting, or getting frantic and nippy from overtiredness rather than calming down. Puppies also need a great deal of sleep, often well over half the day, so rest is as important as activity, and an overtired puppy frequently becomes cranky and bitey. Watch for limping, stiffness, or reluctance to move after activity, which can signal you've pushed too hard or that something needs a vet's look.

Account for your puppy's vaccination status and the weather. Before the vaccine series is complete, keep exercise to safer areas and skip high-traffic dog spots, carrying your puppy through novel environments for socialization rather than walking them through potentially contaminated ground. In hot weather, exercise in the cool of early morning or evening, watch for overheating (especially in flat-faced breeds), check that pavement isn't hot enough to burn paw pads, and bring water. In cold or icy conditions, keep sessions short and watch small or thin-coated puppies for chilling.

Match activity to your individual puppy and increase it gradually as they mature. Breed, size, and personality all affect needs: a working or sporting breed has more drive than a low-energy companion breed, but even high-energy puppies should build up slowly rather than being run hard while young. Once your veterinarian confirms your dog is fully grown and the growth plates have closed, you can gradually introduce longer hikes, running, and dog sports. Until then, consistent, moderate, varied activity, plus plenty of mental enrichment and rest, is exactly what a growing puppy needs.
