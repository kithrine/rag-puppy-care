# Grooming Your Puppy

Start grooming your puppy early, even before they really need it, so they get comfortable being handled. Regularly touching their paws, ears, mouth, belly, and tail, and pairing each touch with treats and praise, makes future grooming and vet visits far less stressful for their whole life. The goal at this age is less about the actual grooming and more about teaching your puppy that being handled all over is normal and pleasant, so that nail trims, exams, and brushing aren't a battle later.

Keep early grooming sessions short, gentle, and rewarding. Introduce tools like the brush, nail clippers, and toothbrush by letting your puppy sniff them and earn treats before you ever use them, then build up in tiny steps. If your puppy gets squirmy or stressed, stop while it's still a positive experience and try again later rather than forcing it through. Many lifelong grooming struggles come from rushing a frightened puppy, while a little patience now pays off for years.

How often you brush depends on your puppy's coat type. Short-coated breeds may need only a quick brush once a week, while long-haired or double-coated breeds need brushing several times a week or even daily to prevent mats and tangles. Curly and continuously growing coats, like those of poodles and doodles, mat especially easily and usually need both regular home brushing and professional grooming every several weeks. Brushing also spreads natural oils through the coat, removes loose hair and dead undercoat, and gives you a routine chance to check for fleas, ticks, lumps, or skin problems.

Bathing should be occasional, not frequent. For most puppies, a bath every few weeks to a couple of months is plenty, or whenever they get genuinely dirty or smelly, because over-bathing strips natural oils and dries out the skin. Use a shampoo made specifically for dogs, never human shampoo, which has the wrong pH for a dog's skin. Keep the water lukewarm, put a cotton ball in each ear or simply avoid getting water in the ears, rinse thoroughly so no residue stays behind, and make the whole thing calm and full of treats. Dry your puppy well afterward, especially in cold weather.

Nail trimming is the task most owners find intimidating, but it matters. Overgrown nails are uncomfortable, can change how your dog stands and walks, and can even curl into the paw pad. Inside each nail is the "quick," a pink area of blood vessels and nerve that you want to avoid cutting; on light nails you can see it, while on dark nails you should trim only tiny slivers at a time and stop when you see a gray or pale circle appear at the cut surface. Trim a little, frequently, rather than a lot at once, and keep styptic powder on hand in case you nick the quick and it bleeds. Getting your puppy used to paw handling early makes this dramatically easier, and a scratchboard or a gradual desensitization plan can help nervous dogs.

Check and clean your puppy's ears regularly, especially for breeds with floppy ears or hair in the ear canal that trap moisture. Use a vet-recommended ear cleaner on a cotton ball or gauze to wipe the parts you can see, and never push a cotton swab deep into the ear canal, which can pack debris or cause injury. Signs of an ear problem include redness, swelling, a bad or yeasty smell, dark or waxy discharge, and frequent scratching or head shaking; if you see those, have your vet check for an infection or ear mites rather than just cleaning.

Wipe and check the eyes and face as needed. Some breeds are prone to tear staining or have facial folds that trap moisture and food, and those wrinkles need gentle, regular cleaning and drying to prevent skin infections. Watch for excessive tearing, redness, squinting, or discharge, which can signal an eye problem worth a vet visit. Trim or keep clear any long hair that falls into the eyes for comfort and vision.

Introduce tooth care as part of grooming, since dental disease is extremely common in dogs and largely preventable. Get your puppy used to having their teeth brushed with a dog-specific toothpaste, never human toothpaste, which can contain xylitol or other ingredients toxic to dogs. Aim for daily brushing if you can, or at least several times a week, and build the habit gradually with rewards. Starting young, while teeth are clean and the routine is new, sets your dog up for far better dental health and fewer costly problems as an adult.

Make parasite checks and prevention a routine part of grooming time. While brushing, run your hands and the comb through the coat to feel for fleas, flea dirt (which looks like black specks), ticks, scabs, or unusual lumps, paying attention to the neck, ears, armpits, and base of the tail. Your vet will set you up with flea, tick, and heartworm prevention and a deworming schedule; staying on top of it protects your puppy and keeps your home from becoming infested.

Learn what your specific breed or coat type needs and plan accordingly. Double-coated breeds should generally be brushed and de-shed rather than shaved, since their coat insulates against both heat and cold. Continuously growing coats need regular haircuts. If you'll use a professional groomer, introduce your puppy to short, positive grooming visits early so the experience is familiar, and choose a groomer who is gentle and patient with young dogs. Whatever the coat, consistent short sessions and lots of praise turn grooming into something your puppy tolerates easily, or even enjoys.
